1. The "99% Purity" Myth

In the pharmaceutical grey market, the term "99% Pure" has lost its meaning. Most Underground Laboratories (UGLs) claim this figure based on raw powder analysis from dubious suppliers, without accounting for degradation during the brewing process or post-production contamination.

The Reality Gap

A 2024 study of 50 UGL samples revealed that while 60% contained the stated active ingredient, over 45% contained heavy metal traces exceeding USP limits, and 15% were underdosed by more than 20%.

True pharmaceutical purity isn't just about the hormone; it's about what else is in the vial. Solvents like Benzyl Benzoate and Benzyl Alcohol are necessary, but in uncontrolled amounts, they cause severe PIP (Post-Injection Pain) and tissue necrosis.

2. HPLC vs. GC-MS: The Gold Standard

To verify authenticity, visual inspection is useless. We rely on two distinct chromatographic methods.

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)

HPLC is utilized to determine the potency of the compound. It answers the question: "Is this 250mg/ml actually 250mg/ml?"

  • Separates components under high pressure.
  • Quantifies the exact concentration of the ester.
  • Detects related impurities from synthesis side-reactions.

Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS)

GC-MS is used for identification. It answers the question: "Is this substance actually Testosterone Enanthate?"

Many "Testosterone" samples in the market are actually cheaper compounds like Propionate labeled as Enanthate, or mixed with fillers. GC-MS creates a molecular fingerprint that cannot be faked.

3. The Silent Killer: Heavy Metals

Often ignored by UGLs, heavy metal contamination comes from poor catalyst filtration during raw powder synthesis. Common offenders include Lead, Arsenic, and Mercury.

Chronic exposure to these metals via intramuscular injection bypasses the liver's "first pass" filter, leading to direct bio-accumulation in kidneys and neural tissue. Shree Tanvika enforces a strict limit of < 0.5ppm for all heavy metals, far below the standard 5ppm allowance.

5. How to Read a Certificate of Analysis (CoA)

A legitimate CoA should always include specific data points. If a supplier sends you a generic "Pass" document, it is likely fake.

What to Look For

  • Batch ID matching your vial
  • Date of Analysis (< 6 months)
  • Specific purity % (e.g., 99.8%)
  • Chromatogram graph attached

Red Flags

  • Word / Excel document format
  • "100%" Purity (Chemically impossible)
  • Blurred or cropped header
  • No lab contact info
